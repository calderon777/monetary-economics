Topic 1 - MCQs Formative Assessment

Author

by Dr Camilo Calderon

How to use this quiz

This quiz is designed as a formative self-assessment tool, not as a test.

Its purpose is to help you: - check your understanding of the key concepts from Topic 1, - identify which types of thinking you find more difficult, and - guide your revision before the tutorial and future assessments.

What this quiz measures

Each question belongs to one of four categories:

  • Subject Knowledge (SK): definitions, concepts, and facts.
  • Analysis (AN): understanding relationships and economic mechanisms.
  • Problem Solving (PS): applying concepts to new situations.
  • Mathematical Thinking (MT): working with ratios, prices, and scaling.

Your score will be broken down by category so you can see where you are strong and where you need more practice.

How to complete the quiz

  • You may use your notes and the lecture materials.
  • Take your time — the goal is understanding, not speed.
  • Use the Hints if you get stuck.
  • You may refresh and retry the quiz as many times as you like.

How to interpret your results

  • A low score is not a problem — it tells you what to focus on.
  • If you score low in:
    • SK: revise definitions and core concepts.
    • AN: revisit the logic of the models and examples.
    • PS: practise applying concepts to new cases.
    • MT: practise the price and ratio calculations.

About the leaderboard

The leaderboard is optional and anonymous. It exists only to make practice more engaging.
It has no effect on your grade and is not used for evaluation.

The only purpose of this quiz is to support your learning.

Subject Knowledge

SK1) Which of the following is not one of the three economic roles of money?
Hint The three roles concern valuation, exchange, and storage — not production activities.
SK2) Which role of money refers to its function as a generally accepted means of payment?
Hint The role that enables buying and selling is the Medium of Exchange.
SK3) Which role of money allows prices to be expressed and compared across goods?
Hint Prices can be compared only if money acts as a common measurement unit.
SK4) Which of the following best defines liquidity?
Hint Liquidity refers to how easily an asset can be exchanged, not to its return or price stability.
SK5) Which monetary aggregate is conventionally defined as “money” in modern economies?
Hint The conventional definition includes assets that can be used directly for payment.
SK6) Which of the following is not one of the four characteristics that underlie liquidity?
Hint Scarcity affects value, not the ease with which an asset can be exchanged.
SK7) Which of the following best explains why money is considered “intrinsically worthless” in modern economies?
Hint “Intrinsically worthless” means money has no direct consumption or production use.
SK8) Which of the following objects is an example of a Unit of Account that is not a Medium of Exchange?
Hint Some units exist only for accounting and pricing, not for everyday transactions.
SK9) Why does money’s role as a Store of Value logically follow from its role as a Medium of Exchange?
Hint Because selling and buying are separated in time, money must store value between them.
SK10) Which statement best reflects the Classical view of money?
Hint The Classical view treats money as neutral with respect to real economic outcomes.

Analysis

AN1) Why does barter require a “double coincidence of wants”?
Hint Trade requires that each party wants what the other offers at the same time.
AN2) Why does the use of money reduce search costs relative to barter?
Hint With money, traders only need to find someone who accepts money, not a specific good.
AN3) Why does the existence of a Unit of Account reduce the informational burden in an economy?
Hint A Unit of Account reduces the number of prices that must be known and compared.
AN4) Why does money’s liquidity allow people to accept a lower financial return on it?
Hint People accept lower returns because liquidity itself is valuable.
AN5) Why does the Classical view regard money as a “veil”?
Hint The Classical view separates nominal prices from real economic outcomes.
AN6) Why does Keynes argue that money has macroeconomic importance beyond facilitating exchange?
Hint Changes in money demand spill over into spending and investment decisions.
AN7) Why is money’s role as Medium of Exchange insufficient on its own to guarantee efficient outcomes?
Hint Even with money, hoarding and coordination failures can prevent efficient outcomes.
AN8) Why is liquidity described as both a property of assets and a property of the monetary system?
Hint Liquidity depends on asset features and on whether others accept it in exchange.
AN9) Why can an increase in the public’s desire for liquidity reduce aggregate demand?
Hint When people hold more money, they spend less, reducing aggregate demand.
AN10) Which of the following best captures the fundamental difference between Keynesian and Classical views of money?
Hint The key difference is whether markets are assumed to coordinate automatically.

Problem Solving

PS1) Two traders meet: one has Apples and wants Bananas; the other has Bananas and wants Apples. What exchange friction is absent in this case?
Hint When each trader wants what the other has, no matching friction arises.
PS2) Why does introducing money allow a trader to accept a good they do not want to consume?
Hint People accept money because it can later be exchanged for desired goods.
PS3) Three traders each prefer a good they do not own. Why does no bilateral trade take place under barter?
Hint If preferences form a cycle, no pairwise trade satisfies both parties.
PS4) Why might a trader refuse to act as a “middleman” by accepting their least-preferred good?
Hint Accepting an unwanted good is risky if it cannot be traded away later.
PS5) Which institutional arrangement could replicate the role of money in a small economy?
Hint Multilateral exchange can replicate money but only if everyone coordinates simultaneously.
PS6) Why does multilateral exchange become impractical as the number of traders increases?
Hint The more participants, the harder it becomes to coordinate simultaneous exchange.
PS7) Suppose people suddenly fear future shortages and increase their money holdings. What immediate effect is most likely?
Hint Holding more money means postponing consumption and investment.
PS8) Which scenario best illustrates a coordination failure rather than a technological failure?
Hint The problem is not production but failure to realise mutually beneficial trades.
PS9) Why can increased liquidity preference cause a recession even when productive capacity is unchanged?
Hint Lower spending reduces output even if productive capacity is unchanged.
PS10) Which policy is most directly aimed at counteracting a surge in liquidity hoarding?
Hint Lower interest rates or higher money supply make holding money less attractive.

Mathematical Thinking

MT1) In an economy with 2 goods, how many bilateral exchange ratios are needed without a unit of account?
Hint With two goods, there is only one pairwise price ratio.
MT2) In an economy with 3 goods, how many bilateral exchange ratios are required without money?
Hint The number of ratios equals the number of distinct good pairs.
MT3) In an economy with 5 goods, how many bilateral exchange ratios are needed?
Hint Use the combinatorial formula for choosing pairs.
MT4) If the price of Apples is \(P_A = 0.5\) and the price of Bananas is \(P_B = 0.25\), what is the exchange ratio of Bananas per Apple?
Hint Exchange ratios are obtained by dividing one price by another.
MT5) If \(P_A = 2\) and \(P_B = 4\), what is the exchange ratio of Apples per Banana?
Hint “Apples per Banana” is the inverse of “Bananas per Apple.”
MT6) An economy has 10 goods. Approximately how many exchange ratios are required without a unit of account?
Hint The number of ratios grows with n², not linearly.
MT7) If the number of goods doubles from 50 to 100, by approximately what factor does the number of exchange ratios increase?
Hint Because ratios grow with n², doubling goods quadruples ratios.
MT8) Suppose there are 700 goods. Approximately how many exchange ratios exist without money?
Hint For large n, n(n−1)/2 is approximately n²/2.
MT9) If \(P_A/P_B = 2\) and \(P_B/P_C = 3\), what is \(P_A/P_C\)?
Hint To combine relative prices, multiply the ratios.
MT10) If an economy introduces a unit of account, how does the informational burden of price knowledge scale with the number of goods \(n\)?
Hint With a Unit of Account, only one price per good is required.
